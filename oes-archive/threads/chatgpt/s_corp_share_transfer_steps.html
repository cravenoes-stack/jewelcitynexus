When your partner wants to exit the S corporation and transfer their 50% ownership to you, there are several legal steps you need to follow to ensure the transfer is properly executed. Here’s an outline of the steps:

1. Review the Shareholders' Agreement

    - Check the Agreement: If your S corporation has a shareholders' agreement, review it for any provisions regarding the transfer of shares. The agreement might outline the process for transferring shares, including any rights of first refusal, approval from other shareholders, or valuation methods.
  • Buy-Sell Agreement: If there’s a buy-sell agreement, it will dictate the terms under which a partner can sell their shares.

2. Valuation of Shares

    - Determine the Value: The shares need to be valued to determine a fair price. This can be done through various methods such as a formal appraisal, agreement between the partners, or according to a formula in the shareholders' agreement.
  • Negotiate Price: Once the value is determined, negotiate the price and terms of payment if the shares are being sold rather than gifted.

3. Draft a Share Transfer Agreement

    - Document the Transfer: A formal agreement should be drafted to document the transfer of shares. This agreement should outline the terms of the transfer, including the number of shares, the price (if applicable), and any other relevant terms.
  • Legal Counsel: It’s advisable to have an attorney draft or review the agreement to ensure it complies with state laws and any corporate governance documents.

4. Execute the Share Transfer

    - Sign the Agreement: Both you and your partner need to sign the share transfer agreement.
  • Endorse Stock Certificates: If physical stock certificates were issued, they should be endorsed by your partner and transferred to you.
  • Update Stock Ledger: The corporation’s stock ledger must be updated to reflect the transfer of shares from your partner to you.

5. Notify the IRS

    - File Form 2553 (If Needed): Although an S corp’s status usually remains unaffected by changes in ownership, you should ensure that the corporation continues to meet the S corporation eligibility requirements. If there are concerns about eligibility, you may need to file or amend IRS Form 2553.
  • Schedule K-1: Your partner should receive a final Schedule K-1 (Form 1120S) reflecting their share of the corporation's income, deductions, and credits up to the date of the transfer.

6. Update State and Local Filings

    - State Filing Requirements: Some states require updates to the ownership records with the state. Check with your state’s business registration office to determine if any forms need to be filed.
  • Amend Operating Agreement: If your corporation has an operating agreement or bylaws, you might need to amend these documents to reflect the change in ownership.

7. Notify Banks and Other Third Parties

    - Bank Accounts: Update the corporation’s bank accounts and financial institutions to reflect the new ownership structure.
  • Contracts: Review any contracts or agreements where the ownership structure is relevant and notify the other parties if necessary.

8. Consider Tax Implications

    - Capital Gains Tax: Your partner may be subject to capital gains tax on the sale of the shares.
  • Gift Tax (If Applicable): If the shares are being gifted rather than sold, consider any gift tax implications. The IRS requires a gift tax return (Form 709) to be filed for gifts exceeding the annual exclusion.

9. Consult with Professionals

    - Legal Counsel: Engage an attorney to guide you through the process and ensure compliance with legal requirements.
  • Tax Advisor: Consult with a tax advisor to understand the tax implications for both you and your partner.
Once all these steps are completed, you will be the sole owner of the S corporation, and the transition will be properly documented and legally sound.

If your partner literally gives you their shares in the S corporation without any payment in return, this is considered a gift under U.S. tax law. Here are the tax implications for you as the recipient:

1. Gift Tax Implications for Your Partner

    - Gift Tax Responsibility: The gift tax is generally the responsibility of the donor (your partner) and not the recipient (you).
  • Annual Exclusion: For 2024, the annual gift tax exclusion amount is $17,000 per recipient. If the value of the shares exceeds this amount, your partner will need to file a gift tax return (IRS Form 709).
  • Lifetime Exclusion: The lifetime gift tax exemption for 2024 is $12.92 million. If the value of the gift exceeds the annual exclusion, it will reduce your partner’s lifetime exclusion amount, but unless your partner’s total lifetime gifts exceed this amount, no gift tax will be owed.

2. Your Basis in the Shares

    - Carryover Basis: When you receive the shares as a gift, your tax basis in the shares is generally the same as your partner’s basis in those shares. This is known as a “carryover basis.”
  • Potential Capital Gains: If you later sell the shares, your taxable gain (or loss) will be calculated based on this carryover basis. If the value of the shares has appreciated since your partner acquired them, you may have a larger capital gain when you sell.

3. No Immediate Income Tax Consequences

    - No Income Recognition: Receiving shares as a gift does not trigger any immediate income tax for you. You will not owe any taxes at the time of the gift.
  • Dividends and Distributions: Once you own the shares, any dividends or distributions from the S corporation will be taxable to you according to the normal S corporation rules.

4. State Tax Considerations

    - State Gift Taxes: While most states do not impose a gift tax, you should verify whether your state has any specific rules or requirements related to the transfer of shares.

5. Recordkeeping

    - Documentation: Ensure that the gift is properly documented with a gift letter or a formal transfer document, and retain records of the original basis of the shares from your partner.

6. Future Tax Planning

    - Estate Planning: Since you now own 100% of the corporation, consider updating your estate plan to reflect this change. It may be beneficial to consult with a tax advisor or estate planner.
In summary, as the recipient of the gift, you do not have immediate tax liability. However, the carryover basis will affect the amount of capital gains tax you might owe if you sell the shares in the future. Your partner is responsible for any gift tax implications, but this will depend on the value of the shares and their lifetime gifting history.
